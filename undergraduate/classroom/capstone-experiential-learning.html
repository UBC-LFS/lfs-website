LFS offers many ways for you to deepen your academic experience. If you're a 3rd- or 4th-year student wishing to clearly demonstrate competency and mastery of your major, or if you just want to enrich your degree with a unique learning opportunity, this page is for you. These opportunities are engineered to help you transition from being a student to the next step in your career, whether that's graduate studies or a smooth entry into the working world.

Undergraduate Thesis

If you're keen on research or intend to pursue a research-intensive graduate program, we encourage you to consider completing an undergraduate thesis. (Available in both the FNH and APBI programs, these courses are numbered 499.) The undergraduate thesis gives you an opportunity to work closely with a faculty member, and to work with the material at an advanced level. This project may be of your own design, or it may be part of your supervisor's ongoing research. Working on an undergraduate thesis will give you an idea of what it's like to work at the graduate level, focusing in depth on a single topic. It will also give you the opportunity to practice and apply your skills in experimental design, data collection and data analysis. And just like a graduate-level thesis, it will equip you with experience in academic writing and oral presentation. You'll be encouraged to present your work at the UBC Multidisciplinary Undergraduate Research Conference (MURC), and you may even be able to submit your work for publication in a peer-reviewed journal. That's a pretty great all-around project for an undergrad!

If the undergraduate thesis appeals to you, we strongly recommended that you complete a research methods class (FNH or APBI 398) beforehand; that will help prepare you for the expectations of this kind of undertaking.

One other thing: if you are in the Honours program for your degree, the undergraduate thesis may be a required element.

Credit value:

All undergraduate thesis courses are worth 6 credits, which means they require a significant investment of time and passion.

Registration and thesis approval process:

It is your responsibility to find an academic supervisor for your thesis, so if you're planning to complete one, we advise you to begin seeking an academic supervisor early! You should contact your designated program advisor for your major as well as the individual faculty member(s) with whom you are interested in conducting a project. Do this well in advance of the start of the academic term. For example, you should approach potential supervisors in the summer regarding projects which would begin in September. Opportunities for conducting projects are limited.

Directed Studies

Directed studies credits (courses numbered 497) are available across all three undergraduate programs (GRS, FNH and APBI). In directed studies, you'll work on a one-to-one basis with a faculty mentor on a research project. Similar to the undergraduate thesis, the end product will be your development and delivery of a research project of your own design, or the research you have done as part of an ongoing project. Most often this work is done within a lab or field setting, but other kinds of projects (e.g. those that are primarily literature-focused) may be approved. Directed studies are a great way to gain valuable research experience. Directed studies that have a small credit value (e.g. 2 or 3 credits) are a less time- and energy-intense commitment than a 6-credit directed study. A 6-credit directed study is appropriate when your project is industry-sponsored and might contain or generate proprietary information, as the final report will not be a public document like a thesis. Your final report will give you valuable experience with scientific writing — and you may get to hone your public speaking skills with an oral presentation.

Credit value:

Normally, directed studies courses are worth 3 or 6 credits, but it's possible to also pursue a 2-credit version.

Registration and directed studies approval process:

It is your responsibility to find an academic supervisor for your directed studies. If you're planning to undertake directed studies, start seeking an academic supervisor well ahead of when you want to begin. You should contact your designated program advisor as well as the individual faculty member(s) with whom you are interested in conducting a project far in advance of the beginning of the academic term. For example, you should approach potential supervisors in the early summer regarding projects which would start in September. Opportunities for conducting projects are limited.

Undergraduate Essay

Available in the APBI program only, the undergraduate essay (APBI 498) allows you to complete a comprehensive and analytical literature review of an approved topic under the supervision of a faculty member. If you take this route, you will typically complete your essay in the final year of your program. The topic may be one that you have a particular interest in, or you may develop a topic under the advisement of your supervisor.

The undergraduate essay is like a "thesis lite"; you get to explore a topic of deep interest, without the hands-on lab/field component of a thesis.

Credit value:

Undergraduate essay courses are worth 3 credits, and require a significant investment of time and passion.

Registration and essay approval process:

It is your responsibility to find an academic supervisor for your undergraduate essay. If you're planning to go this route, start seeking an academic supervisor early! Get in touch with your designated program advisor for your major as well as the individual faculty member(s) with whom you are interested in conducting a project well in advance of the beginning of the academic term. For example, you should approach potential supervisors in the early summer regarding projects which would start in September.

You can learn more about expectations and timelines right here.

Practicums & Internships

The Applied Biology Practicum (APBI 496) and LFS Career Development Internship (LFS 496) are ways for you to gain practical experience in your coursework. In these courses, you will work side-by-side with faculty mentors to develop and deliver your project(s).

If you want to gain experience in potential fields of future employment working with organizations or companies related to your major, the APBI practicum will be of interest to you.

The LFS Career Development Internship (LFS 496) is open to all students in all programs. It's an excellent opportunity for you to earn credits while being engaged with a mentor in a local company whose business is food system-related. It's also a great opportunity for you to get your hands dirty with existing projects at the UBC Farm. Many of these projects include the opportunity to conduct original research with supervision. Check out the listings of available internships and timelines for applications at the Centre for Sustainable Food Systems at UBC Farm. You can also use your own connections to find an internship placement with a local business or organization.

Credit value:

You can earn a maximum of 6 credits of internships, either as one 6-credit option or two 3-credit projects.

Registration and approval process:

You can apply in February, July and November for internships that start in May, September and January. Get in touch with your designated program advisor for your major to see how an internship can fit into your program of study. The time commitment is generally one of:

  • three months, 9h/week;
  • six months, 9h/week; or
  • three months, 18h/week.

You can learn more about expectations and timelines right here.

International Field Studies

What better way to develop a personalized understanding of land and food systems issues than by expanding your learning to a different country? Build your initiative, independence and adaptability — career skills that are prized by employers — by studying at an international university or conducting research in another location.

When you register in courses numbered LFS 302A, B, C or D (not all sections are offered each year), you will work alongside a small group of peers to experience hands-on learning as led by one of our LFS faculty. Each course is unique in location, length, and cost (including travel to and from the experience, lodging, food etc.), so be sure to think about your financial capacity before committing to a course like this.

International field studies are a great way to develop those competencies that enhance your employability! Everything you need to know about the diverse options in international learning can be found right here.

Credit value:

International field studies courses are worth 3 or 6 credits.

Registration process:

You should contact the Global Partnership Coordinator in LFS, Roxana Quinde, about applying for a field study course. Typically, you'll need to register for your chosen field study several months prior to the actual course dates. Courses are offered in summer and winter terms. You will also need to confirm with your program advisor how the credits will be applied to your program. You can find your program advisor's name on the webpage for your major.

Questions?

If you have questions, we encourage you to talk to Roxana or to your program advisor to discuss how international field studies could fit within your program requirements.

Summer Abroad Programs

Our Faculty maintains partnerships with several universities around the globe that offer summer programs for which you can earn program credits. Summer Abroad is a great way to earn credits while enjoying an immersive cultural experience in another country, be it France's wine regions, taking in Copenhagen, or in the jungles of Costa Rica. Programs differ in length, start date and number of credits that can be transferred back to your program. Often you'll even have the opportunity to complete a practicum after the course to deepen your experience. Some programs offer partnership prices, which make them an especially economical way to travel and earn credits at the same time.

Credit value:

You'll need to check out each program to see how many credits you can earn, as this can vary from 3 to 12. (European program credits are given in ECTS (European Credit Transfer and Accumulation System); just remember, two ECTS = one UBC credit!)

Registration and approval process:

You will need to plan ahead and register for any summer programs by February 1. Make sure that you have talked with the Global Partnership Coordinator in LFS, Roxana Quinde, and your program advisor about applying earned credits toward your UBC program.

To learn more about expectations and timelines:

Check out UBC's Go Global site for more information. LFS and Go Global also hold Summer Abroad information sessions in January of each year.

LFS Core Series

As an LFS student, you'll take at least two courses from the LFS core series ( LFS 100 and LFS 250) as part of your degree requirements. Depending on your program of choice, you may also be required to take LFS 350, with LFS 450 as an option. (LFS 350 and LFS 450 are recommended as electives in all programs that don’t already include them.)

The LFS core series embraces a different kind of learning, where assessment by your instructors is based more on process than on outcomes. You'll be working on big issues, where ambiguity and uncertainty are part of the natural terrain, and where there's not necessarily a "right" answer. If you're used to multiple-choice thinking, you're about to experience a whole new way of showing what you know.

In the core series, you'll work collectively to solve problems and address issues. You'll be presenting and speaking with groups more often than you've done before. You'll be writing more. You'll be learning how to be accountable not just to yourself, but to the other people you're connected with on a project, from peers to instructors to community partners. And you'll be shaping all that learning not just off-site, in the community, and in small groups that go beyond the traditional lecture/lab structure, but inside a flipped-classroom paradigm, where you're no longer just a knowledge consumer but are increasingly a knowledge creator.

The LFS faculty has shaped this educational approach over a number of years, and it's backed by some pretty powerful research. This way of learning is very reflective of how things actually work in the real world — and that's what we're getting you ready for. Our work here in LFS is to equip you with knowledge, but it's also to educate professionals who are ground-ready to cope with the demands of a variety of different careers. As part of your studies, we incorporate the practical experiences that are increasingly required in the career sphere — things like building your network outside the UBC community and collaborating on interdisciplinary projects through community-based experiential learning (CBEL). No matter your background or discipline, the LFS core series primes you to enter the workforce as a competent, inquiry-minded and collaborative professional.

Land One

If you want to take your classes with a familiar cohort of peers in first year, Land One is perfect for you. This limited-enrolment first-year option is jointly offered with the Faculty of Forestry. This unique way of experiencing first year lets you take your core first-year courses (math, biology, economics and communications) in an integrated format to deeply explore real-world issues from both Indigenous and Western perspectives.
